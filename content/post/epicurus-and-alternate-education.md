Education has always been associated with a good career, wealth, cultural and societal status. Do well in school to get into a good college, then do well in college to get a good job, and finally work hard to build a long career and become rich. Many of us go through these stages without understanding ourselves, our character, purpose or role in this world, all of which shapes who we truly are.

While reading [How to Do Nothing: Resisting the Attention Economy](https://www.amazon.in/How-Do-Nothing-Resisting-Attention-ebook/dp/B07FLNFRGK) by Jenny Odell, I came across [The Garden of Epicurus](https://wiki.epicurism.info/Epicurus%27_Garden/) and its role as a resistance to the mainstream education offered then, such as in Plato's Academy and Aristotle's Lyceum. This sparked my curiosity, and I found myself following a trail of crumbs that felt oddly familiar in a modern sense.

## Epicurus' Garden

Around 306 BCE, Epicurus, an independent philosopher, became dissatisfied with the politics and mainstream education in Athens and decided to do something different.

After the demise of Alexander the Great, Athens became a battlefield for power and ambition. The philosophy and [[education]] then leaned towards producing good statesmen rather than teaching the principles for living an examined, fulfilling life.

Epicurus deemed this state of warfare as the major source of anxiety and suffering for the people. He decided to retreat from public life and purchased a private space away from the city to do something different. This space would eventually come to be known as *Epicurus' Garden* or *The Garden*, and become the fertile ground where his philosophical ideas would take root.

## Philosophy

Epicurus wanted to create a school that would optimize not for political ambition, but for a sense of happiness and peace of mind. His goal was to cultivate a state of mind called *Ataraxia*: a state of being free from mental disturbance, anxiety, and fear.

Some of the philosophical ideas of this institution or community included:
1. **Community**: Live together, share resources and help each other grow
2. **Inclusivity**: Welcome people from all backgrounds, from the elite to the enslaved
3. **Detachment**: Avoid public life, politics, and ambition
4. **Conversations**: Engage through conversations, not lectures

## Curriculum

The curriculum discussed through conversations included:
1. **Empiricist theory of knowledge**: Use one's senses, preconceptions formed from patterns, and feelings to perceive and reason about the world. Since the judgement of our senses can be faulty, supplement it with rational thinking
2. **Physics**: The Universe consists of atoms moving in empty space. Everything we know is made from atoms. Epicurus used this theory to remove the fear of death and divine punishment — the primary causes of anxiety then — by providing a natural explanation for all phenomena
3. **Ethics**: Learn how to live well
    1. Attain a state of Ataraxia by embracing pleasure and avoiding fear
    2. Cultivate friendships as a source of long-term pleasure and happiness
    3. Strive for the simple life
    4. Recognize that pain is usually manageable
    5. Avoid the political life, which he considered the primary source of unhappiness in people

## Parallels with Alternate Education Today

The philosophy and teachings of Epicurus share similarities with alternative education models today, such as Montessori and Waldorf:

1. **Curiosity as the driver**
    - Use curiosity as the guiding light to explore and learn new things.
    - Learn new things using the senses from everyday experiences
2. **Community as the source of support and comfort**
    - Foster community not hierarchy. Mix with people from different age groups and from different walks of life
    - Foster collaboration over competition and make learning a shared effort
    - Find support and comfort in the community for emotional growth
3. **Learning as living**
    - Education is not a ladder to someone else's expectations. It's a way of learning how to live a good life
    - Joy is a sign of meaningful learning and not a distraction
    - A calm and simple environment is a catalyst for learning
4. **Freedom and Independence**
    - Avoid fear-based discipline
    - Support individual paths of learning. Don't teach a monkey and a fish how to climb a tree



Much of conventional schooling today is shaped by patterns inherited from the Industrial Revolution. As I learned more about The Garden, I began wondering what our world might look like if Epicurean philosophy had shaped mainstream education instead. If the quality of education and economic growth of a nation go hand-in-hand, would such a shift have fundamentally changed India's trajectory toward becoming a developed nation?
